# Chile Public Sector Balance Sheet: Research Findings

## Date: 2026-03-22

### Summary of Key Findings

Chile does NOT publish a single, fully consolidated public sector balance sheet that brings
together ALL assets AND liabilities of the entire public sector into one document. However,
multiple institutions produce partial balance sheet data that, taken together, cover most of
the picture. The IMF has constructed a more comprehensive estimate through its Fiscal
Transparency Evaluation (2021) and its PSBS database.

---

## 1. Does Chile publish a single consolidated document covering BOTH assets AND liabilities?

**SHORT ANSWER: No -- not a single fully consolidated document for the entire public sector.**

Chile publishes several partial documents from different institutions:

### A. Contraloria General de la Republica (CGR) - "Informe de Gestion Financiera del Estado" (IGFE)

- Published annually (by April 30 each year)
- Covers 697 entities: 286 public services, 345 municipalities, 32 state enterprises/CORFO institutes, 33 higher education institutions, and the Central Bank of Chile
- Reports budgetary, financial, AND patrimonial (assets/liabilities/equity) situation
- HOWEVER: Each chapter is an independent financial statement - there is NO consolidation across subsectors
- Individual entity balance sheets exist but they are NOT aggregated into one single consolidated balance sheet

### B. DIPRES (Direccion de Presupuestos) - "Informe de Finanzas Publicas" (IFP)

- Published quarterly
- Covers the Central Government only
- Reports: Gross Debt, Financial Assets of the Treasury, and Net Financial Position (PFN)
- PFN = Gross Debt minus Treasury Public Investments
- As of 2025: PFN was approximately -37.9% of GDP
- Gross Debt: ~41.7% of GDP (2025)
- Does NOT cover the full public sector (excludes Central Bank, SOEs, universities)

### C. DIPRES - "Anuario de Estadisticas de las Finanzas Publicas" (EFP)

- Annual yearbook covering 10-year periods
- Covers Central Government, General Government, and Public Enterprises
- Includes financial assets and liabilities data
- Follows IMF GFSM 2001/2014 framework
- Includes separate spreadsheets for financial assets and liabilities

### D. Banco Central de Chile - "Cuentas Nacionales por Sector Institucional" (CNSI)

- Quarterly publication
- Covers all institutional sectors: Households, Non-Financial Companies, Financial Companies, Government, Rest of World
- Includes current accounts, accumulation accounts, and financial statements (balance sheets)
- Covers the General Government sector
- Provides: Balance of Financial Assets and Liabilities, Net Acquisition of Financial Assets, Net Contracted Liabilities

### E. Ministry of Finance (Hacienda) - "Informe de Estadisticas de la Deuda Publica"

- Disaggregated information on financial assets and liabilities
- Covers: Central Government, Central Bank, and Public Enterprises

## 3. What accounting standards does Chile use?

Chile uses an **indirect adoption of IPSAS (known locally as NICSP)**:

- **CGR Resolution No. 16 (February 16, 2015)**: The Contraloria General issued national accounting standards based on IPSAS/NICSP
- **Indirect adoption**: Chile does not adopt IPSAS verbatim; instead, the CGR issues national standards that comply with IPSAS requirements but are adapted to Chilean organizations
- **41 IPSAS standards** were considered (as issued by IFAC up to 2019)
- **Scope**: Applies to all entities regulated by the CGR, including central government (centralized and decentralized), and municipalities
- **GFSM framework**: Fiscal statistics follow IMF GFSM 2001/2014 standards
- **SDDS Plus**: Chile completed IMF SDDS Plus adherence in March 2020
- **GAP**: IPSAS 32 (Service Concession Arrangements) and PPP-related standards are only partially implemented; PPP liabilities reported at only 0.2% of GDP

## 4. IMF, World Bank, and international organization publications

### A. IMF Fiscal Monitor (October 2018) - "Managing Public Wealth"

- **Chile IS included** in the PSBS (Public Sector Balance Sheet) database
- The database covers 31 countries (61% of global economy); broader database covers 38 with full public sector data, 75 total
- Chile is among the Latin American countries analyzed alongside Brazil, Colombia, and Argentina
- Data available at: <https://data.imf.org/Datasets/FAD_PSBS>

### B. IMF Fiscal Transparency Evaluation (2021) - Country Report No. 21/241

- **This is the most comprehensive external assessment of Chile's public sector balance sheet**
- Key finding: Total net worth including main unreported items = **26.8% of GDP**
- Unreported items that would INCREASE net worth:
  - Copper reserves: +24.9% of GDP
- Unreported items that would DECREASE net worth:
  - Pension liabilities: -17.5% of GDP
  - Debt at market value vs face value adjustment: -5.7% of GDP
- Chile scored Good or Advanced on 21 of 36 fiscal transparency principles
- Available at: <https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/CR/Issues/2021/11/11/Chile-Fiscal-Transparency-Evaluation-506777>

### C. IMF Government Finance Statistics (GFS)

- Chile reports to the IMF GFS database
- Balance sheet data (assets and liabilities) is included
- Chile adheres to SDDS Plus since March 2020
- Data available at: <https://data.imf.org>

### D. World Bank

- The PSBS database is an IMF product, not World Bank
- World Bank's Prosperity Data360 platform mirrors the IMF PSBS data
- No independent World Bank "Public Sector Balance Sheet" for Chile was found
- The World Bank maintains a Research and Development Center in Chile

## 5. Academic papers and think tank reports

### A. Levy Yeyati & Sturzenegger (2007/2023) - "A Balance-Sheet Approach to Fiscal Sustainability"

- Originally a Harvard CID Working Paper No. 150 (2007)
- Published in Fiscal Studies, Vol. 44(1), pp. 61-84 (March 2023)
- **Constructs a public sector balance sheet for Chile (2005 data)**
- Uses stochastic modeling with 5,000 simulated economic paths
- Computes net worth as net present value of fiscal flows
- Analyzes sensitivity to copper price shocks (42% decline scenario)
- Available at: <https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1475-5890.12319>

### B. Marcel (2010) - "The Structural Balance Rule in Chile: Ten Years, Ten Lessons"

- IADB publication
- Discusses how the structural balance relates to net worth variation of central government

### C. Fuentes - "Fiscal Rule and Public Investment in Chile"

- IADB publication examining fiscal frameworks and balance sheet implications

### D. BIS Papers No. 20 - "Fiscal rule and central bank issues in Chile"

- Examines interactions between fiscal and monetary policy balance sheets

### E. Convergence Study: Colombia and Chile NICSP comparison

- Academic paper comparing NICSP implementation models in Colombia and Chile
- Available on Redalyc

## 6. What is the Contraloria's "Balance del Sector Publico" - does it truly cover both sides?

### The IGFE Structure

- The IGFE (Informe de Gestion Financiera del Estado) is the closest thing to a comprehensive public sector financial report
- It covers **697 entities** across all subsectors
- It reports on the **budgetary, financial, and patrimonial** situation
- Individual entities DO prepare full balance sheets (estado de situacion financiera) with assets, liabilities, and equity under NICSP/IPSAS

### CRITICAL LIMITATION (per IMF 2021 FTE)

- **"The financial statements cover close to 100% of the public sector entities BUT there is NO consolidation (nor aggregation) of subsectors"**
- Each chapter is an independent financial statement
- No single consolidated Estado de Situacion Financiera exists for the whole sector
- The Central Bank of Chile and Banco del Estado are NOT covered in fiscal statistics (though they appear in the IGFE)
- Public universities are also a gap in fiscal statistics

### What IS consolidated

- DIPRES fiscal statistics DO consolidate the **General Government** (central government + municipalities)
- Public corporations data is included separately
- But Central Bank and Banco del Estado remain excluded from these consolidations

### Recent quality concerns

- In 2025, the CGR abstained from approving financial statements of 73 public entities (50 central government, 23 municipalities) covering 2020-2024
- Total resources in observations: US$ 17.5 billion
- Reasons: lack of analysis, weak internal controls, inconsistent accounting records

## KEY CONTRARIAN FINDINGS

1. **The popular narrative** says Chile has strong fiscal institutions and transparency. The data confirms this is PARTIALLY true -- Chile scores well on IMF transparency codes -- but reveals a significant blind spot: there is NO single consolidated public sector balance sheet.

2. **The hidden wealth**: When copper reserves (24.9% of GDP) are properly valued, Chile's net worth jumps dramatically. But this is almost entirely offset by unreported pension liabilities (17.5% of GDP). The conventional focus on gross debt (~42% of GDP) dramatically understates Chile's true fiscal position in both directions.

3. **Institutional fragmentation**: At least 5 different institutions produce partial pieces of the balance sheet puzzle (CGR, DIPRES, Banco Central, Hacienda, SEP). No one entity "owns" the consolidated picture.

4. **NICSP/IPSAS adoption is incomplete**: Despite the 2015 resolution, implementation remains problematic. The CGR's refusal to approve 73 entities' financial statements suggests the accounting infrastructure is not yet mature enough for reliable consolidation.

5. **The CFA warning**: Treasury assets have collapsed from 12% of GDP (2011) to 5.3% (2021), while gross debt has risen from ~9% to ~42% of GDP. The net financial position has swung by approximately 32 percentage points of GDP, from creditor to debtor.
